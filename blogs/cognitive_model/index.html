∫ Toward Human Cognition from Language Models (#private)

Kernel:

An attempt to reconcile language models with our thinking model.

  • Augment LMs with goals
  • Make models that incrementally learn
  • Make models hierarchically decomposable
  • Memory tagging beyond sequence encoding via Hippocampus

All points are informally validate through experiments.

Understading how we think is always an elusive problem that has been around since the dawn of human civilization. The attempt, successful or not, led to the creation of human marvels like the calculator, the computer, and the AI. Today, we are living in the era where the language model AIs, especially very large ones, are integrating into our daily lives. Those who are enthusiastic about the AI, among which I am one, are excited about the potential of these models, and what can we do to make them even more human-like.

In this blog, I would like to informally discuss my thoughts on the matter. If you would care to read, I promise you will find the ideas somewhat interesting.

∂ Models that pursue goals

The language models are autoregressive models that predict the next token given the previous tokens. Unlike them, humans do possess agendas (if you believe that there's something going on in other people's minds). And because we have different agendas, we act differently under the same circumstances. This call for a change in the basic equation of the language models.

Given the state \(s\) and the next state \(x\), the autoregressive models update have the following form:

$$ \begin{align*} s \leftarrow \arg\max_{x} \Pr(x | s) \tag{1} \end{align*} $$

Let's define agenda or goal or target as \(t\). To incorporate the goal \(t\), we need to change the equation to:

$$ \begin{align*} s \leftarrow \arg\max_{x} \Pr(t | x) \Pr(x | s) \tag{2} \end{align*} $$

One might argue that the goal could be encoded in the state \(s\) and would yield the same results. But as I'm going to show you, it's more efficient for us to build models that explicitly separate the goal from the state.

∂ Incrementally learnability

Equation (1) has the following definition: \( S \times T \to S \) which requires at least \( \Omega(|S||T|) \) data points to represent the exact function. The equation (2) has the following definition: \( S \to S \) and \( T \to S \) which requires only \( \Omega(|S|) \) data points to represent both functions. Fewer required data points to represent the exact functions immediately imples that we also need less data to learn the approximators of the functions. Furthermore, when training models, stochastic gradient descent is the de facto method. But there is not much evidence that learning in the brain uses back propagation. Back propagation also has issues with online learning due to catastrophic interference. I believe (this is not science but art) that we perform Hebbian tying. There're plenty of this kind of learning observed in the brain, especially in basal ganglia and motor neuron. Mathematically, back propagation is like computing approximate correlation of deep function, same with Hebbian learning. When we perform learning, we do not make a forward-backward pass through lots of layers. but a very few layers. In reality, training neural network requires a lot of effort to tune the hyperparameters. Incremental learning is known to be hard, and does not yield the best outcomes at the moment. I've been struggling to train even the basic linear model with toy parameters. The fact that the transformers were invented and the author managed to pull it off are nothing short of a miracle and truly deserve to be called a breakthrough in the field of AI. One things we can do here is to break down multi-layer model into mini-sub models. By sub-modularizing the model, not only that we make it easier to train, but we also make it easier to interpret.

∂ Hippocampal tagging for relative retrieval

Relative, mutable memory is a key to human cognition. What did you eat yesterday? The last time you met your friend, what did you talk about? Or when performing calculation in head, we often has to refer back to the previous calculation results to perform the next calculation. Classical models are not capable of this. But the language models can do this thanks to the position encoding. Position encoding is a way to tag the memory with the sequence of the input data. To enable models the sense of relative time. What more? When you are in a room, how do you know the position of the nearest exits? How do you perform dead reckoning? and estimate the distance you have walked in the area you are not familiar with? https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integration When playing chess blindly, how do you keep track of all similar pieces (like pawns) on the board? ∂ Experiment

Optimal vs hierarchy vs random action

In this experiment, I compare the performance of the optimal routing, the goal-pursue hierarchy model, and the random routing. The optimal routing is the routing that minimizes the distance between the start and the goal. The goal-pursue hierarchy model is the model that uses the goal-pursue model to infer the next action. The random routing is the routing that randomly selects the next action. From the experiment, we can see that the goal-pursue hierarchy model performs better than the random routing while does not require computation complexity as the optimal routing.

Guiding language model generation

In this experiment, I try to guide text generation of a small language model using the training data from a large language model. You can see that the small model can generate text as good as the large one even with fewer parameters and less capacity to store general knowledge. This is a bit cheating though. First of all, generation is run on the same domain of the training data. Second, the small model does not operate alone but requires the entire goal-pursue hierarchy to generate the text. This contributes to the parameter count. But the goal of this experiment is to show that we can control the language model given a trained goal-pursue hierarchy. This opens suggest an alternative structure of the language model, compared to the de facto standard of the stacked decoder only transformers.

Reinforcement learning

In this experiment, I try to train solve the classic cartpole problem using the goal-pursue hierarchy model. The goal is to balance the pole on the cart. Surprisingly, the model can learn to balance the pole on the first try without any hyperparameter tuning. Even I was surprised by the result.

∂ Final remarks

There're gaps that have not been bridged yet. For example, the action of attention shifting from internal to external cannot be learned via demonstration. It has to be reinforced through external rewards. This also includes motor motions.
